Assessment Objectives – H474

Every mark on every component is awarded against these five objectives. Know them and use them to frame your analysis.

AO1 Communicate clearly — apply relevant concepts and terminology from literary and language study. Quality of argument and expression.
AO2 Analyse language, form, and structure — apply linguistic and literary frameworks to explain how meaning is made. The core analytical skill.
AO3 Contexts — explore connections between texts and the contexts in which they were produced and received. Historical, social, biographical, generic.
AO4 Connections between texts — explore relationships and comparisons between texts. The comparison and synthesis skill across C01, C02, and NEA.
AO5 Creative and critical writing — demonstrate understanding of literary and language concepts through your own writing. Applies to C01 Section A re-creative element and NEA.

Component 03 – NEA

Independent Study: Language Investigation + Creative Writing with Commentary. Worth 20% of H474.

Language Investigation
1,750–2,500 words · AO1, AO2, AO3
An original investigation into a language topic of your choice, with data, methodology, and analysis using linguistic frameworks.
Creative Writing + Commentary
750–1,000 words + 500-word commentary · AO1, AO5
An original creative piece demonstrating understanding of literary and language methods, plus a reflective analytical commentary.

Language Investigation

A systematic, data-driven investigation into a language question. You choose the topic, collect data, and analyse it using the linguistic frameworks from the course.

What makes a good investigation topic?

1

It must be a genuine question

Not "I am going to describe how teenagers speak" but "How do modal verbs differ in frequency and function between formal and informal teen speech?" A question has a hypothesis you can test.

2

It must be manageable with real data

You need to collect or locate primary data: transcripts, adverts, social media posts, speeches, newspaper articles, song lyrics. Aim for a corpus you can actually analyse in depth — 500–1,500 words of data is usually sufficient.

3

It must connect to linguistic frameworks

Your analysis must apply AO2 frameworks: lexis, grammar, phonology, pragmatics, discourse. A good topic is one where these frameworks reveal something interesting — not just confirm the obvious.

4

It should connect to AO3 (context)

The best investigations situate their findings in a broader linguistic or social context: gender and language, power and politeness, language change, dialect variation, media representation, identity and code-switching.

Topic ideas by area

Language and gender
How do modal verbs differ in men's and women's speech? Do political speeches by male and female politicians use hedging differently?
Language and power
How is authority constructed through syntax in court proceedings? How do CEOs and politicians differ in their use of passive voice?
Language change
How have semantic fields in newspaper headlines shifted across two decades? How have food menus changed in register over time?
Media and representation
How do tabloid and broadsheet newspapers construct the same news event differently through lexical choice? How is mental health represented in magazine discourse?
Identity and dialect
How do speakers code-switch between standard and non-standard English in different contexts? How does the written dialect of social media vary across communities?
Spoken language
How do discourse markers differ between formal interviews and informal conversation? How is disagreement managed differently in face-saving strategies across contexts?

Investigation structure

1. Introduction (10–15%) — State your research question and hypothesis. Why is this topic worth investigating? What do you expect to find and why?
2. Methodology (10–15%) — How did you collect or select your data? What are the limitations of your approach? What linguistic frameworks will you apply and why?
3. Analysis (50–60%) — Apply linguistic frameworks systematically to your data. This is the heart of the investigation. Quote from your data; use terminology precisely; link findings to your hypothesis. This is not a list — it is analytical argument.
4. Conclusion (10–15%) — What did you find? Does the data support or complicate your hypothesis? What are the limitations? What further research would be valuable?
5. Data appendix — Include your raw data. It does not count towards the word limit.

Common mistakes to avoid

– Describing language rather than analysing it (AO2 failure)
– Not having a genuine research question — just a topic
– Collecting too much data and analysing it superficially
– Forgetting AO3: situating your findings in linguistic theory or social context
– Using only one or two linguistic frameworks when more apply
– Writing a conclusion that simply summarises — it must evaluate and reflect

Creative Writing (750–1,000 words)

An original creative piece that demonstrates your understanding of how language and literary methods construct meaning. This is AO5: it must show craft, not just creativity.

What the creative piece must do

Demonstrate literary craft — deliberate choices of form, structure, narrative voice, imagery, and style. Not just "a good story" but a piece that shows you understand how language creates meaning.
Show linguistic awareness — your choices of lexis, syntax, register, and discourse structure should be purposeful and analysable.
Be original — not pastiche or imitation. Your own voice, your own ideas.
Connect to AO5 — the mark scheme assesses how well you demonstrate understanding of literary and language concepts through your own writing.

Form options

Short fiction
A self-contained short story or extract. Consider: narrative perspective, implied reader, structural choices, how you open and close.
Personal essay / memoir
A lyric essay, memoir extract, or personal narrative. Consider: the constructed "I", distance between writer and narrator, register choices.
Poetry
A sequence of poems or a single extended poem. Consider: form, line breaks, voice, sound, white space — all the tools of the set texts.
Dramatic monologue
A voice piece — theatrical, confessional, or satirical. Consider: who speaks, to whom, what is concealed.

What makes a top-grade creative piece

– A distinctive, controlled voice — not generic
– Purposeful structural choices: why does the piece start and end where it does?
– Deliberate lexical choices: every significant word should be analysable in the commentary
– A sense of form that earns its shape — free verse that earns its freedom, structure that earns its regularity
– Imagery that works: concrete and specific, not vague and decorative
– A piece that rewards re-reading: ambiguity, layers of meaning, structural echoes

Commentary (500 words)

The commentary is not a summary of your creative piece. It is an analytical, critical reflection on the language and literary choices you made — and why.

What the commentary must do

Analyse your own choices — explain specific decisions about lexis, syntax, form, structure, voice, and imagery. Quote from your own piece and analyse it as you would a set text.
Use AO2 frameworks — apply the same linguistic and literary terminology you would use in the exam. The commentary is an opportunity to demonstrate explicit knowledge of how language works.
Reflect on effect — what effect were you aiming for? How did your choices create that effect? What would have been different if you had chosen differently?
Connect to wider reading — you can reference influences: set texts, other writers, linguistic theories. Show that your piece exists in relation to a literary and linguistic tradition.

Commentary structure (500 words)

Opening (50–70 words): State the form and intention of your piece. What were you trying to achieve? What voice or perspective did you construct and why?

Analytical body (350–400 words): 3–4 focused analytical points, each referencing a specific language or literary choice. Structure: choice made → quotation from your piece → explanation of effect → link to intention. Apply AO2 terminology throughout.

Closing reflection (50–70 words): What were the challenges? What would you do differently? What does the piece do that you are most satisfied with, and why?

Common mistakes

– Summarising the story rather than analysing the writing
– Writing "I used alliteration to make it sound nice" — no analysis of effect or intention
– Not quoting from your own piece
– Ignoring linguistic frameworks and writing as if this were a pure literature commentary
– Being vague: "I wanted to create atmosphere" — what specific choices created it?

NEA Mark Scheme & AOs

Language Investigation — AOs assessed

AO1 (33%) — Quality of argument, use of linguistic terminology, clarity and organisation of writing. Your investigation must be clearly structured and precisely written.
AO2 (33%) — Application of linguistic frameworks to your data. This is the analytical core: lexis, grammar, phonology, pragmatics, discourse applied rigorously to your corpus.
AO3 (33%) — Contextual understanding. How do your findings connect to the social, historical, or theoretical context of language use? What do they reveal about language in society?

Creative Writing + Commentary — AOs assessed

AO1 (50%) — Quality of the creative writing itself: originality, control of voice, clarity of expression. The commentary is also part of AO1: how well do you communicate analytically about your own choices?
AO5 (50%) — Demonstrated understanding of literary and language concepts through your own creative writing. Does the piece show that you understand how language constructs meaning? Is your craft informed by what you have studied?

Band descriptors (summary)

Band 4 (top): Perceptive, detailed analysis with precise linguistic terminology. Investigation has a clear methodology and situates findings in a well-developed theoretical context. Creative writing is distinctive, controlled, and shows sophisticated understanding of how language works. Commentary analyses choices analytically with specific reference to the piece.

Band 3: Thorough analysis with mostly accurate terminology. Clear methodology. Creative writing shows confident control of voice and form. Commentary explains choices clearly.

Band 2: Relevant analysis with some accurate terminology. Some awareness of context. Creative writing is competent but undistinctive. Commentary describes rather than analyses.

Band 1: Limited analysis. Terminology imprecise or absent. Little contextual awareness. Creative writing is generic or poorly controlled. Commentary is largely summary.
